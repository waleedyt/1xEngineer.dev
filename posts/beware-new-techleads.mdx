Becoming a Team Lead? Congratulations! If you are new to the role, gear up for one of the toughest challenges you would face with a high risk of transformation into a better engineer and leader. Becoming an engineering team lead for the first time is like being swarmed with the debris in outerspace 👾. It's frightening yet exciting experience, and after the survival, you will find new worlds to explore. 🚀   

Questions you need to ask yourself to prepare for the challenges:
### 1. How to balance between doing it yourself and delegating? 
It's easy to overwork yourself and similarly its easy to lose touch by not doing enough! 
One should always be on a beware mode in this regard! Ideally, you shouldn't be spending more than 30% of your time managing stuff; otherwise, there won't be enough time to work on sizeable deliverables to push your engineering limits. Similarly, you don't want to be overworking yourself. Your team's growth enablement is your responsibility, and it needs dedicated time and mindshare.  
However, it's okay to slide a bit on either side of the spectrum for one-off situations or events. Some good examples would be performance reviews where you would spend more team as a team lead or a critical problem impacting users, demanding more engineering time. 

### 2. How do you delegate and assign work? 
It is easy to assign people work from their comfort zones to boost productivity. It indeed results in temporary productivity boost but it also stands in the way of enabling team members who aren't too comfortable right now and have untapped potential.   
Doing more work is the recipe to get more experience and become more productive. The bigger picture to consider here is to enable everyone per their strengths and truly become a 10x team. You can be intuitive about it, but it's almost always better to find a systematic approach to improve methodically as you get more insights. For example, rotating the modules every sprint or after two sprints. 

### 3. How to balance between features and housekeeping? How to onboard the stakeholders?
Setting the priority of housekeeping is problematic because it can be tricky to justify the value it brings. However, it's important to remember that housekeeping is vital for keeping the codebase productive and contributing teammates happy. Similarly, a crappy codebase can become a primary reason for your team members to leave. Please see this [post](https://www.1xengineer.dev/posts/technical-debt-showdown) for longer and a more detailed insights on the topic.

### 4. How to define performance metrics for your team members? 
It's easy to go overboard when defining goals centered around business and product. You need to align the personal development as well. Finding the sweet spot is extremely hard and needs continuous iterative improvements with your team members. Get started with a good enough set and improve along the way.

### 5. How to measure the performance of your team members?
Be ready to give people feedback without any sugar coating. It’s hard but there is no other way around it. It could be a career-saving favor from you to someone.
Performance reviews are a world on their own. Organizations may execute it via an automated tool, but still, it's imperative to understand the philosophy and do them the right way. I am a huge fan of Gergely Orosz's work on the topic; please see this post on [Pragmetic Engineer](https://blog.pragmaticengineer.com/performance-reviews-for-software-engineers/) for more details.

### 6. How to manage your growth as an engineer and a Leader? How to become more effective? 
It’s hard to manage the product backlog, team and team's performance, and one's individual contributions. The only way to cope without burning out is to be really really effective.   
It’s very easy to blunt your engineering skills on the job while trying to be a better lead. Finding the right balance between the two is very hard. As a lead, it's easy to identify some aggressive management milestones and postpone the balance. It's important to realize that it's not about a certain milestone. The world changes fast, so you will face new challenges every day, and your goals will change over time as well; hence it's possible that you may never reach an optimal point where everything just fits in the balance. It's a marathon not a sprint, so a certain milestone doesn't matter if you start doing little things and make progress every mile.    
You’ll have to work on becoming more and more effective because you have to become good at both sides of speectrum to be an effective lead.
Tip; read Effective Engineer, in fact, read it at the start of every year! It's a perfect reminder for the job. 

### 7. How to make your job easier?
Empower people around you. It is the only way to make your job easier overtime. Be ready to strategically empower people so that you have minimal dependencies. And of course, get ready for doing 1-1s over lunch 🍕!
Be ready to give people wins to position them as winners.   
Tip; Sometimes, it's okay to be humble and even give fabricated wins to people to give them a taste for it. We all know how addictive success can be 😃.   
Be ready to think about people’s careers, opportunities for them, and help them improve.  
Tip; Find and ask the right questions, make people think for themselves since you only have limited brain cycles to spend.
